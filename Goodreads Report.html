The Nordic Theory of Everything: In Search of a Better Life Finnish-born journalist Anu Partanen compares the lives of Finns and Americans across the areas of education, health, work, social security, and business. The idea underpinning the book is the ‘Nordic theory of love’. Contrary to misplaced notions that Nordic countries are socialist, this is a philosophy in which individuals are supported by the state so as not to be dependent on it or the other people around them; in other words, these countries bake in liberty from the moment their citizens are born by allowing their parents time off and equipping them with free education and healthcare. />/>The problem with comparing the USA to anywhere else is that it is an outlier to begin with. So of course the book tends to take the format of “look how bad life is in the USA… but look how much better it is in Scandinavia” (though Partanen does acknowledge that Nordic countries are far from perfect, and that the American outlook can have its advantages). />/>I was most interested in the section on education, for instance: how Finland reformed its education in the 1970s in anticipation of a knowledge economy; how it takes a supply- rather than demand-side approach to education (it’s a basic human right, not a good driven by demand); and how Finnish teachers are so well respected because of the sheer amount of training they have to undertake (700 hours of classroom practice during a master’s program). There were also interesting references to Beatrice Webb and the role of her idea of a “national minimum of civilised life” in founding Britain’s welfare state. />/>Altogether though, the book probably should have been cut by at least a third as it’s rather repetitive. There was also too much focus on America – this was necessary due to the comparison, but I felt like I finished the book wanting more insight into Nordic society and culture, not the societal ills of the USA (with which I’m already sadly well-acquainted). 3
